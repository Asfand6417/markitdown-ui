My MPhil in Computer Science at the University of Haripur, Pakistan, was built entirely around
multi-view markerless kinematics. My thesis developed a dual-camera pipeline for 3D human
pose reconstruction: synchronized video capture, 2D keypoint detection using MediaPipe’s deep
learning backbone, and 3D reconstruction via Direct Linear Transformation across calibrated
camera views. The system was validated against a Vicon marker-based motion capture reference
, 15 subjects, over 4,000 annotated frames , and achieved a Mean Per Joint Position Error of
8.77±1.72mm. I also built a quality assurance framework for the kinematic dataset: inter-rater
reliability assessment using Cohen’s (0.84 for phase labelling, 0.79 for fault identification), one-
κ
way ANOVA with Tukey post-hoc testing across joint categories, and 95% confidence intervals
computed using t-distribution across all validation metrics. A biomechanical fault detection
module analysed joint angles against clinical thresholds and achieved 85.3% agreement with
certified domain experts. A manuscript based on this work is currently under review at Scientific

The primary role in this position , contributing to the development and validation of multi-view
and monocular markerless motion capture pipelines, and building a quality assurance framework
for kinematic datasets , is a precise description of what my thesis delivered. That isn’t a
coincidence I’m reaching for; it’s a direct match. What changes in the CEREBRIS context
is what matters most: the movement being analysed is upper-limb stroke recovery, and the
downstream consumer of the kinematic output is not a sports coach but a clinician making
rehabilitation decisions. That shift in stakes changes the standard to which the system must
be held. It demands the same rigour I applied in my validation work , but now with clinical
interpretability, regulatory awareness, and genuine patient outcomes on the line.
That is the research direction I want to pursue. I am specifically drawn to LLUI’s model
of ubiquitous rehabilitation , aligning interventions with a patient’s actual clinical state and
movement data rather than fixed protocols. The CEREBRIS consortium’s goal of reaching
Technology Readiness Level 4 by project completion means this PhD isn’t purely theoretical; it’s
building something that will be evaluated in real clinical settings. Working within a consortium
of 14 European partners also means genuine interdisciplinary collaboration with clinicians,
engineers, and translational researchers , the kind of environment where a computational
background like mine has to translate its outputs into something a physiotherapist can actually
use.
My technical stack is Python-first, with hands-on experience in PyTorch, TensorFlow, OpenCV,
NumPy, scikit-learn, and Matplotlib for machine learning, computer vision, and statistical
data analysis. I have direct experience building multi-view synchronisation pipelines, designing
kinematic feature extraction modules, and validating system outputs against reference standards
with full statistical reporting. I am comfortable with Git-based collaborative development,
Linux environments, and structured documentation for research pipelines , relevant to the
containerisation and version control practices the role notes as desirable. I have working
knowledge of MATLAB, and my experience with real-time pipeline optimisation on GPU
hardware applies directly to the performance requirements of a deployable markerless capture
system. I understand what it means to build something not just for a paper but for eventual
clinical use , where reliability, interpretability, and failure modes matter as much as accuracy
metrics.
My long-term goal is to develop biomechanical assessment systems that work reliably outside

afterward. The AxoMotris position offers exactly the right combination of technical depth,
